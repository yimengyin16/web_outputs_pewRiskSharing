1 Overview of key risk-sharing features

The South Dakota Retirement System (SDRS) adopted a contingent COLA policy in response to the Great Recession. In 2017, South Dakota approved changes which, along with other funding arrangements of SDRS, aim to achieve budgetary certainty for the sponsoring governments, funding sufficiency for the plan, and retirement adequacy for retirees.

The current SDRS policy includes the following fundamental features:

  • Fixed, statutory member and employer contribution rates. Currently, both employee and employer contributions of SDRS are fixed at about 6 percent of payroll.
  • Flexible benefits that change based on economic conditions and the SDRS funded status. A contingent COLA is the most important component of the flexible benefit arrangement.
  • If COLA adjustments cannot achieve full funding while keeping employer contributions in a stable target range, the policy requires corrective actions. Those actions are not fully spelled out, and might include benefit reductions, contribution increases by employers or employees, or other actions by the legislature or the retirement system. While this feature does not require specific actions, it is a formal statement that action must be taken.

The SDRS policy seeks to keep employer contributions stable and stay fully funded by adjusting the COLA whenever possible. However, the COLA cannot be reduced below 0.5 percent or raised above 3.5 percent. It will not always be possible to achieve these goals, and the policy therefore requires a corrective action plan without defining what that plan must do.

In this policy, plan retirees bear almost all risk through COLAs that are linked to the plan funding status, unless the underfunding gap becomes so severe that corrective action is triggered and requires the employer or active plan members to share the risk.

2 Summary of modeling approach

2.1 Policy scenarios

To examine the impact of the SDRS risk-sharing features on plan costs, funded status, and member benefit, we run the simulation under the following three policy scenarios.

  1. SDRS policy, cost of corrective action borne by employer

    • It is assumed that addtional contributions are required when corrective action is triggered.

    • The additional contributions are soly made by employer

  2. SDRS policy, cost of corrective action shared between employer and employees

    • It is assumed that addtional contributions are required when corrective action is triggered.

    • The additional contributions are evenly split between employer and employees

  3. A DB plan with the same benefit rules as SDRS but without the SDRS risk-sharing policies

Additional variants will be examined in future analysis.

2.2 Investment return scenarios

  1. Stochastic: 6.75% expected mean return, 12% standard deviation. This is just for purposes of initial analysis. Will be replaced by scenarios and capital market assumptions provided by Pew.

2.3 Model assumptions and Simplifications

  • There are two tiers in SDRS: Foundation (pre-2017) and Generational (post-2017). Currently the benefit rules for Foundation members are applied to all members. We will model the two tiers seperately in later versions.

  • Benefit rules for Foundation members:

    • Normal retirement age: 65

    • Benefit factor: 1.7% for service before 2008 and 1.55% for service after 2008.

    • Range of COLA: 0.5% to 3.5%

  • Key valuation methods and assumptions:

    • Discount rate: 6.5%

    • Cost method: EAN

    • Baseline COLA: 2.25%

    • Asset smoothing: No (market value)

    • Amortization of UAAL in policy scenario 3: 20-year level-percent closed amortization
